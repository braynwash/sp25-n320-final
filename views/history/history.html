Snake Milking: A Brief History

Snake milking is the process of extracting venom from snakes, and it has a long and fascinating history. The practice dates back to ancient times when people recognized the medicinal properties of snake venom. However, it wasn't until the late 19th century that snake milking became a formalized practice for medical purposes.

The first recorded instance of snake milking for medical purposes occurred in 1895 when a French scientist named Albert Calmette developed the first antivenom using venom from the Indian cobra. This breakthrough led to the establishment of snake farms and laboratories dedicated to venom extraction and research.

Another one of the most famous early snake milkers was Ross Allen, who made a name for himself in the 1920s by performing public demonstrations at his Reptile Institute in Florida. Allen helped show the world that with the right training, technique, and a lot of guts, people could safely harvest venom for important scientific work. Over time, snake milking grew into a real profession, supplying venom to labs, universities, and pharmaceutical companies. Today, milking snakes is still critical, not just for making antivenoms, but also for developing new medicines.
